üîÑ What is the Product Life Cycle?

  • Theory that all products follow a similar pattern over time
  • Five key stages: Development ‚Üí Introduction ‚Üí Growth ‚Üí Maturity ‚Üí Decline
  • Sales patterns change predictably at each stage
  • Helps businesses plan marketing strategies effectively

Academic Definition: The product life cycle shows the sales of a product over time. It describes the stages a product goes through from when it was first thought of until it finally is removed from the market. Not all products reach this final stage - some continue to grow, and others rise and fall.

The PLC is an important concept in marketing that enables businesses to understand market dynamics and adjust their strategies accordingly.

  • Development: Few users, early adopters, minimal competition
  • Introduction: Building customer base, establishing brand, pricing strategy
  • Growth: Growing adopters, competitor entry, fight for market share
  • Maturity: Market saturation, maintaining share, emphasis on efficiency
  • Decline: Sales drop-off, competitor exit, selective distribution

When new products launch, sales are usually slow. Customers are unaware, distribution is limited, and marketing costs are high relative to sales.

If successful, products enter growth phase. Sales accelerate as awareness builds, repeat purchases occur, and word-of-mouth spreads.

Eventually, sales stabilize at maturity. Most potential customers have been reached, competition intensifies, and market is saturated.

Finally, decline sets in. Newer alternatives emerge, customer preferences shift, or technology becomes obsolete.

üöÄ Introduction Stage

üí°‚û°Ô∏èüì¢
  • Low sales volume - customers unaware of product
  • High costs per unit - low production volumes, high marketing spend
  • Basic product versions - likely to be simple, core features only
  • Limited distribution - testing customer reactions and retailer interest
  • Pricing strategy critical - skimming (high price) or penetration (low price)

Promotion: Marketing aims to raise awareness and explain product benefits. Advertising is informative rather than persuasive. Early discussions with retailers help finalize product packaging and positioning.

Distribution: May be limited as distributors wait to see customers' reactions. Businesses use this stage to gather feedback before wider rollout.

Financial Reality: This stage rarely generates profit. High development costs and overheads are spread over relatively few units. Launch costs and promotional expenses are substantial.

‚öñÔ∏è Maturity Stage

üìä‚û°Ô∏èüí∞

Portfolio Management: Businesses may diversify their range, focusing on most profitable items and removing poorly-performing variants that don't sell well.

üìâ Decline Stage

üìä‚¨áÔ∏è
  • Sales falling - customer interest decreasing
  • Reduced competition - some competitors exit the market
  • Lower marketing spend - costs reduced as product phased out
  • Selective distribution - focus on key profitable channels
  • Discount pricing - clearing inventory, maintaining some sales

Why Decline Happens: Technology advances, customer preferences change, better alternatives emerge, or fashion/trends move on.

Divestment Decision: Lower budgets to keep costs down. Eventually, decision must be made whether to discontinue entirely or reinvent/reposition the product.

Focused Distribution: May focus on key outlets and more profitable channels only, abandoning less profitable retail relationships.

The Four Ps Must Adapt: Marketing managers need to adjust their marketing mix at different stages of the product life cycle to optimize performance and profitability.

Introduction Focus: High investment in product development but only prototypes produced. Sales are zero, costs per unit high. Not needed to set price yet. Promotion may begin to start raising customer awareness before launch.

Decline Management: Still likely to be low as development costs have been covered and reduced promotional costs are needed to raise awareness. Focus on most profitable items and channels.

üîÑ Extension Strategies

üìà‚è∏Ô∏èüîÑüìà
  • Definition: Marketing plans to extend the maturity stage before decline
  • Purpose: Delay decline and extend profitable period
  • Timing: Implemented during late maturity, before sales drop
  • Goal: Cheaper than developing entirely new products
  • Success varies: Some strategies work brilliantly, others fail

Strategic Importance: Extension strategies are marketing plans to extend the maturity stage of the product before a brand new one is needed. They aim to give existing products a new lease of life.

Cost Effectiveness: Extending an existing product is usually cheaper and less risky than developing a completely new product from scratch. Development costs have already been recovered.

Market Reality: However, many extension strategies fail. Consumer preferences may have fundamentally shifted, or competitors may have superior alternatives that extension strategies cannot overcome.

Without Extension: Sales would naturally decline (shown as dotted line). With successful extension strategies, sales can be maintained or even increased temporarily.

Rebranding: Changing packaging, logos, or marketing messages to appeal to different customers or refresh tired brand image. This can make old products feel new again.

üìä Product Portfolio Analysis

üì¶üì¶üì¶üì¶
  • Definition: Analyzing the range of existing products to allocate resources effectively
  • Purpose: Examine existing position and plan what to do next
  • Key Question: Which products deserve investment? Which should be discontinued?
  • Balanced Portfolio: Mix of products at different life cycle stages
  • Cash Management: Profitable products fund development of new ones

Strategic Purpose: Product portfolio analysis examines the existing position of a firm's products. This allows the firm to consider its existing position and plan what to do next.

Resource Allocation: Not all products deserve equal investment. Some generate cash that can fund development of others. Portfolio analysis helps make these decisions systematically.

Risk Management: Having products at different life cycle stages reduces risk. If one product fails or declines, others can sustain the business.

Strategic Planning: Analyzing the range of existing products of a business helps allocate resources effectively between them and guides decisions about new product development.

‚≠ê The Boston Matrix (BCG Matrix)

‚≠êüêÑ‚ùìüêï
  • Purpose: Framework for analyzing product portfolio systematically
  • Two Dimensions: Market growth rate (vertical) and market share (horizontal)
  • Four Categories: Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs
  • Strategic Tool: Guides investment and divestment decisions
  • Portfolio Balance: Ideally, business has products in multiple categories

Developed by: Boston Consulting Group in the 1970s, hence "BCG Matrix" or "Boston Matrix"

Market Growth Rate (Vertical Axis): Indicates how fast the overall market is expanding. High growth markets offer opportunities; low growth markets are mature or declining.

Relative Market Share (Horizontal Axis): Measures the product's market share compared to competitors. High share indicates market leadership; low share suggests competitive disadvantage.

Strategic Quadrants: Each quadrant suggests different strategic actions - invest, maintain, develop, or divest. Products move between quadrants over time as markets and competitive positions evolve.

‚≠ê Rising Stars

‚≠êüìà
  • High market growth + High market share
  • Market leaders in expanding industries
  • Require investment to maintain position against competitors
  • Generate revenue but need cash to fund growth
  • Future potential: Can become Cash Cows when market matures

Strategic Approach - HOLDING: This involves marketing spending to maintain sales and is used with rising star products. The goal is to protect market leadership as the market grows.

Investment Requirements: Stars require continued investment in marketing, production capacity, and product development to defend their position against competitors attracted by the growing market.

Cash Flow: Stars generate substantial revenue due to high market share, but this is often reinvested to maintain competitive position. They are typically cash-neutral.

Future Trajectory: As market growth slows, Stars should transition to Cash Cows, maintaining high share in a now-mature market with reduced investment needs.

üêÑ Cash Cows

üêÑüí∞üí∞üí∞
  • Low market growth + High market share
  • Dominant position in mature, stable markets
  • Generate high profits with minimal investment needed
  • Fund other products - provide cash for Stars and Question Marks
  • Defend position but don't invest heavily in growth

Strategic Approach - MILKING: This means taking whatever profits you can without much more new investment and is often used with cash cow products. Maximize cash extraction while maintaining market position.

Profit Characteristics: Cash Cows have achieved economies of scale, have established distribution networks, and require minimal marketing spend. Most revenue flows straight to profit.

Portfolio Role: The cash generated by Cash Cows funds development of Question Marks and supports Stars. Without Cash Cows, businesses struggle to invest in future growth.

Maintenance Strategy: Sufficient investment to maintain market share and defend against competitors, but avoid expensive growth initiatives. Focus on efficiency and cost management.

‚ùì Question Marks (Problem Children)

‚ùìü§î
  • High market growth + Low market share
  • Uncertain future - could succeed or fail
  • Require significant investment to build market share
  • Consume cash without generating much revenue yet
  • Critical decision: Invest heavily or divest?

Strategic Approach - BUILDING: This involves investment in promotion and distribution to boost sales and is often used with problem children (question marks). The goal is to convert them into Stars.

The Dilemma: Question Marks are in attractive, high-growth markets but lack market share. Heavy investment might turn them into Stars, but failure means wasted resources.

Investment Requirements: Need substantial cash for marketing, R&D, production scaling, and distribution expansion. This cash usually comes from Cash Cows.

Decision Point: Management must decide: invest aggressively to gain share, or divest and redirect resources elsewhere. Maintaining low investment is usually worst option - neither succeeds nor exits.

Alternative Name: "Problem Children" reflects the challenge they present - demanding attention and resources with uncertain outcomes.

üêï Dogs

üêïüìâ
  • Low market growth + Low market share
  • Weak position in unattractive markets
  • Generate little profit or may lose money
  • Tie up resources that could be better used elsewhere
  • Typical action: Divest or discontinue

Strategic Approach - DIVESTING: This involves selling off the product and is common with dogs or problem children. Cut losses and reallocate resources to more promising products.

Why They Exist: Former Stars or Cash Cows that declined, or Question Marks that failed to gain traction. Markets matured or shrank while competitive position weakened.

Resource Drain: Dogs consume management attention, occupy shelf space, use working capital, and tie up production capacity without generating adequate returns.

Exceptions: Some Dogs are kept for strategic reasons - completing a product line, serving loyal niche customers, or blocking competitors. But most should be eliminated.

Exit Challenges: Discontinuing products can damage customer relationships, leave employees jobless, or create negative publicity. Must be managed carefully.

Ideal Portfolio Balance: A healthy business should have a mix of products across categories. Cash Cows fund development, Stars provide future Cash Cows, selected Question Marks offer growth potential, and Dogs are minimized.

Cash Flow Dynamics: The portfolio should be self-sustaining. Cash Cows generate surplus funds that finance Stars (to maintain position) and Question Marks (to build share). Dogs should be eliminated to stop cash drain.

Lifecycle Connection: Products move between categories over time. Question Marks that succeed become Stars. Stars become Cash Cows as markets mature. Cash Cows eventually become Dogs if not refreshed. This is why continuous innovation is essential.

Strategic Timing: Invest in Question Marks before competitors dominate. Maintain Stars before they lose position. Milk Cash Cows before they decline. Divest Dogs before they destroy too much value.

üí° New Product Development (NPD)

üí°‚û°Ô∏èüî¨‚û°Ô∏è‚úÖ‚û°Ô∏èüöÄ
  • Definition: Process of turning new ideas into market-ready products/services
  • Involves: R&D, market research, engineering, design, packaging, branding
  • Critical for survival: Replaces products in decline, maintains competitiveness
  • High risk: Most new products fail (70-90% failure rate)
  • Essential investment: Companies without NPD become obsolete

Formal Definition: New product development includes the people and processes involved in turning new ideas into products or services ready for launch.

Cross-Functional Activity: NPD involves research and development (R&D), market research, product engineering and design, plus expertise in packaging, advertising, price and branding.

PLC Connection: NPD represents all the activities categorized as 'development' in the product life cycle. It's the foundation for future growth.

Why Necessary: Markets evolve, customer preferences change, technology advances, and competitors innovate. Without NPD, businesses rely on declining products and eventually fail.

üîÑ The NPD Process

  • 1. Idea Generation: Creating and collecting new product concepts
  • 2. Screening: Filtering ideas to identify most promising
  • 3. Concept Development: Developing detailed product concepts
  • 4. Business Analysis: Evaluating financial viability
  • 5. Prototype Development: Creating working samples
  • 6. Test Marketing: Limited launch to gauge market appeal
  • 7. Commercialization: Full-scale launch

Product Development Process: A formal process of generating, selecting, developing, and commercializing product ideas. Each stage acts as a filter, eliminating weak ideas before significant investment.

Idea Generation Sources: Customer feedback, employee suggestions, competitor analysis, R&D breakthroughs, market research, technological advances.

Screening Criteria: Market potential, technical feasibility, strategic fit with business, competitive advantage, resource requirements, profit potential.

Risk Reduction: Each stage reduces risk before major investment. Better to kill ideas early when costs are low than after spending millions on development.

Screening: Cadbury team selected 3 finalists from thousands

Test Marketing: Public vote to choose winner, limited production

Commercialization: Winner's bar launched nationally

üîß Prototypes

üî®‚û°Ô∏èüì¶‚û°Ô∏èüß™
  • Definition: Pre-production samples used for testing and evaluation
  • Purpose: Test functionality, design, manufacturing before full production
  • Identify problems: Find and fix issues cheaply before mass production
  • Gather feedback: Show to customers, retailers, get reactions
  • Refine design: Make improvements based on testing results

Academic Definition: Prototypes are pre-production samples of products used for testing and evaluation. They bridge the gap between concept and commercial product.

Testing Purposes: Functional testing (does it work?), durability testing (will it last?), safety testing (is it safe?), user experience testing (is it easy to use?), manufacturing feasibility (can we make it economically?).

Cost Efficiency: Finding problems at prototype stage costs thousands. Finding same problems after launch costs millions in recalls, reputation damage, and redesign.

Iteration: Multiple prototype versions are common. First prototype reveals issues, second prototype addresses them, third prototype refines further. Process continues until ready for production.

Stage 3: First physical prototype - basic materials, test structure

Stage 4: Athlete testing - Cristiano Ronaldo, Serena Williams test boots

Stage 5: Feedback - too heavy, uncomfortable, grip issues identified

Stage 6: Revised prototypes - materials changed, design adjusted

Stage 7: Final prototype approved after 10-20 iterations

Stage 8: Manufacturing setup for mass production

Process takes 18+ months before consumers can buy the shoe

üß™ Test Marketing

  • Definition: Selling product on limited basis to gauge market appeal
  • Limited geography: One city, region, or country before full launch
  • Real conditions: Actual stores, real prices, genuine marketing
  • Gather data: Sales volumes, repeat purchases, customer feedback

Academic Definition: Test marketing is the stage of product development in which a product is sold on a limited basis to gauge its market appeal. It's a real-world experiment before full commercialization.

What's Tested: Price sensitivity (will people pay this?), purchase frequency (will they buy again?), marketing effectiveness (does advertising work?), distribution feasibility (can retailers stock it?), competitive response (how do rivals react?).

Geographic Strategy: Choose representative market - similar demographics to target market but isolated enough that failure won't damage national brand reputation.

Risks: Competitors see your product and can copy or prepare counter-strategies. Test market failure can create negative publicity. Some businesses skip this stage and launch nationally.

üìà Three Key Uses of PLC

  • 1. Planning Marketing Mix: Adjust product, price, promotion, place at each stage
  • 2. Cash Flow Forecasting: Predict when investment needed vs. when profits generated
  • 3. Portfolio Balance: Ensure products at different stages to maintain stability

Marketing Mix Decisions: Introduction needs awareness advertising; Growth needs distribution expansion; Maturity needs differentiation; Decline needs cost reduction. PLC guides these decisions systematically.

Cash Flow Management: Introduction and Growth consume cash (investment exceeds revenue). Maturity generates cash (revenue exceeds costs). Decline may still generate some cash but declining. Helps plan financing needs.

Portfolio Planning: Recognising the need for a balanced product portfolio. Don't want all products declining simultaneously - need mix of introduction, growth, and maturity products to ensure stable cash flow and sustainable growth.

Strategic Timing: Knowing which stage products are in helps time new product launches, extension strategies, and divestment decisions appropriately.

üéÆ Real Example: PlayStation Strategy

PS VR2 (Introduction): Heavy investment, building awareness, uncertain future

PS6 (Development): R&D investment, no revenue yet

Cash Flow: PS4 profits fund PS5 growth and PS6 development. Balanced portfolio ensures continuous income while investing in future.

Without this balance, would either have no growth products or no cash to fund them.

‚ö†Ô∏è Product Life Cycle Limitations

  • Not all products follow pattern: Some skip stages, others stay in maturity indefinitely
  • Hard to predict timing: Can't tell how long each stage will last
  • Self-fulfilling: Treating product as "declining" can cause decline
  • Varies by industry: Fashion has short cycles, pharmaceuticals have long cycles
  • Extension strategies change shape: Successful extensions mean cycle doesn't follow theory

Theory vs Reality: PLC is a model - useful for understanding patterns but not every product fits perfectly. Some products never reach introduction, others skip growth, some stay mature for decades.

Timing Uncertainty: Biggest limitation is can't tell how long stages last. One product might stay in growth for 2 years, another for 20 years. Makes planning difficult.

Management Influence: If managers believe product is declining and cut marketing, it will decline - becomes self-fulfilling prophecy. Product might have been saved with investment.

Industry Differences: Fast fashion: 3-month cycles. Pharmaceuticals: 20-year patent protection. Industrial machinery: 50+ year lifecycles. Single model can't capture all variations.

Lesson: With sufficient investment and strategy, maturity stage can last almost indefinitely. PLC is guide, not law.
